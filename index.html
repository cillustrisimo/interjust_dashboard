What is universal jurisdiction?

Understanding the laws that enable States to fight atrocity crimes, and their context.

A joint project: Berkeley Responsibility Lab × Project Meridian

While -- U.N. Member States have laws that allow them to investigate and prosecute crimes committed beyond their own borders, few have actually done so in practice.[1]

Project Meridian is dedicated to mobilizing States to use and improve their laws to pursue accountability for the most serious international crimes, so that all survivors can realize meaningful access to justice and perpetrators have nowhere to hide.

Universal jurisdiction is a legal principle which allows States to prosecute the most serious international crimes -- war crimes, crimes against humanity, genocide, and the crime of aggression -- regardless of where those crimes were committed, the nationality of the perpetrator, and the nationality of the victim.[2] Lawyers, civil society actors, and other advocates have been working to develop universal jurisdiction as a mechanism to pursue justice for survivors of mass atrocities since the Second World War.[3] Despite some setbacks, the application of universal jurisdiction has been gaining momentum over the past three decades.[4] The timeline below marks major events in the development and application of universal jurisdiction.

What types of crimes can States prosecute under universal jurisdiction?

States can prosecute the most serious international crimes -- war crimes, crimes against humanity, genocide, and the crime of aggression -- under universal jurisdiction. In order for a State to prosecute one or more of those crimes under universal jurisdiction, the State must first criminalize them in its national laws.

Who can be held responsible under universal jurisdiction?

Under universal jurisdiction, States can prosecute direct perpetrators of atrocity crimes as well as individuals who had control over the direct perpetrators. States can hold the latter category of persons liable for the most serious international crimes under the doctrine of command or superior responsibility.

Figure 3: Command or Superior Responsibility – -- U.N. Member States recognize command or superior responsibility as a form of liability in their criminal code.

In the context of situations like Ukraine, where the International Criminal Court is pursuing a handful of top-level individuals and Ukrainian prosecutors cannot target most commanders due to the previous absence of command responsibility in Ukrainian criminal law, the use of command or superior responsibility in cases launched in other national jurisdictions that recognize the legal doctrine can fill an important gap in accountability efforts.

Case Study

Russian Mercenary Commander Yan Petrovsky Prosecuted in Finland for War Crimes Committed in Eastern Ukraine

Yan Petrovsky is a Russian national and co-founder and former commander of Rusich, a far-right mercenary unit linked to the Wagner Group.[21] In September 2014, Rusich mercenaries under Petrovsky's command took part in an ambush in Luhansk, eastern Ukraine that left 22 Ukrainian soldiers dead and four others injured.[22] In 2023, Petrovsky, then living under the alias Voislav Torden, circumvented a Schengen Area entry ban and travelled to Finland, where he obtained a one-year residence permit based on family ties.[23] The Finnish Border Guard arrested Petrovsky during a security check at Helsinki Airport in July 2023 and ascertained his real identity through database checks.[24] Finland's National Bureau of Investigation led a ten-month investigation into Petrovsky's alleged involvement in war crimes in eastern Ukraine, in close cooperation with Ukrainian authorities, beginning in December 2023.[25] The Helsinki District Court convicted Petrovsky of war crimes in March 2025.[26] Petrovsky's conviction is the first worldwide under the principle of universal jurisdiction for war crimes committed in Ukraine.[27]

What are the different ways States can investigate and prosecute crimes that occurred outside their own borders?

States can invoke several types of jurisdiction to investigate and prosecute crimes that were committed outside their own borders. With the exception of absolute universal jurisdiction, each of these bases for jurisdiction relies on the existence of some type of "link" between the prosecuting State and the crime committed, such as the nationality of the alleged perpetrator or victim, or the presence of the alleged perpetrator on the prosecuting State's territory. In order for a State to investigate and prosecute extraterritorial crimes, the State must first pass a law that recognizes one or more of these jurisdictional bases.

Absolute universal jurisdiction is the "purest" form of universal jurisdiction, reflecting the original idea behind the principle that certain crimes are considered to be committed against the international community as a whole. In many States, laws granting jurisdiction over the most serious international crimes committed abroad are subject to a significant limitation: the suspected perpetrator must be physically present in that State to trigger jurisdiction. This limitation, commonly referred to as "conditional universal jurisdiction,"[34] is often motivated by resource constraints which justify focusing on those perpetrators who are actually present on the would-be prosecuting State's territory.

Most States exercise jurisdiction over crimes committed by their nationals, regardless of the place of commission. This is commonly referred to as "active personality" jurisdiction. Some States interpret this type of jurisdiction to include alleged perpetrators who are residents, refugees, or stateless persons residing permanently in that State.

Case Study

Former ISIS Member Taha al-Jumailly Prosecuted in Germany for Genocide, Crimes against Humanity, and War Crimes Committed Against Yazidi Women in Iraq

Taha al-Jumailly is an Iraqi national and former ISIS member who committed genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes through his enslavement and abuse of a Yazidi woman and her daughter.[28] Along with his wife, Jennifer Wenisch, a German national who was later convicted of crimes against humanity and war crimes, al-Jumailly held the Yazidi woman and her daughter in inhuman conditions at his home in Fallujah during the summer of 2015.[29] The German Federal Court of Justice issued an arrest warrant for al-Jumailly in April 2019, based on a structural investigation into crimes committed against the Yazidi by ISIS in Syria and Iraq that German authorities had opened in 2014.[30] Greek authorities arrested al-Jumailly based on the European Arrest Warrant in May 2019, and extradited him to Germany in October.[31] The Higher Regional Court of Frankfurt am Main convicted al-Jumailly of genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes and sentenced him to life imprisonment in November 2021.[32] Al-Jumailly's genocide conviction was the first worldwide of an ISIS member.[33]

Many States exercise jurisdiction over crimes committed against their nationals, regardless of the place of commission. This is commonly referred to as "passive personality" jurisdiction. Some States extend this jurisdiction to victims who are residents, refugees, or stateless persons residing permanently in that State.

Case Study

Former Gambian Interior Minister Ousmon Sonko Prosecuted in Switzerland for Crimes Committed in The Gambia During the Regime of Yahya Jammeh

Ousmon Sonko is a Gambian national who committed crimes against humanity in several roles under the regime of Yahya Jammeh, who led The Gambia from 1996-2017.[35] Sonko left the Gambia in September 2016.[36] Trial International filed a criminal complaint with prosecutors in Switzerland in January 2017, after it became aware of Sonko's presence in country.[37] Switzerland's Office of the Attorney General then opened what would become a six-year investigation which involved multiple trips to The Gambia to collect evidence and hear witness testimony.[38] In 2021, The Gambia's Truth, Reconciliation, and Reparations Commission recommended that Sonko be prosecuted for killings and acts of torture and sexual violence committed between 2000 and 2016.[39] The Federal Criminal Court of Bellinzona convicted Sonko of crimes against humanity and sentenced him to 20 years' imprisonment in May 2024.[40] Having previously served as The Gambia's Minister of the Interior, Sonko is to date the highest-ranking State official prosecuted by a European court under the principle of universal jurisdiction.[41]

Many States are required to exercise jurisdiction over crimes committed abroad as an obligation under a treaty. Most relevant for Project Meridian, the 1949 Geneva Conventions, which govern the conduct of war and provide protections for those who are not taking part in hostilities, include an obligation to prosecute suspected war criminals or extradite them to a State that is better placed to prosecute.[54] Therefore, any State that has ratified the Geneva Conventions has not only jurisdiction but an obligation to investigate and prosecute "grave breaches" of the Conventions committed in other States.

Some States can exercise jurisdiction over crimes that affect their sovereignty, security, or national interest, or simply over crimes that they consider to be committed against their country. This is commonly referred to as "protective principle" jurisdiction. While this type of jurisdiction could in theory extend to the most serious international crimes which are seen to affect the interests of all countries,[52] States rarely invoke it to investigate and prosecute such crimes.[53]

Case Study

Assad Regime Officials Prosecuted in Absentia in France for the Arrest, Detention, and Disappearance of Dual French and Syrian Nationals in Damascus

Ali Mamlouk, Jamil Hassan, and Abdel Salam Mahmoud are all Syrian nationals and former high-ranking officials in the Assad regime who were connected to the November 2013 arrest, detention, and disappearance of dual French and Syrian nationals Patrick and Mazen Dabbagh in Damascus.[47] Obeida Dabbagh, a family member of the disappeared, filed a complaint at the Paris Tribunal with the International Federation for Human Rights and one of its French affiliates in October 2016.[48] Judges from France's specialized unit for the prosecution of international crimes issued international arrest warrants for the three suspects in October 2018, and they completed their investigation in March 2022.[49] The Paris Criminal Court convicted Mamlouk, Hassan, and Mahmoud in absentia for complicity in crimes against humanity and war crimes and sentenced them to life imprisonment in May 2024.[50] In its ruling, the Court held that functional immunities for State officials do not apply in serious international crimes cases.[51]

Case Study

Dual Ethiopian and Dutch National Eshetu Alemu Prosecuted in the Netherlands for International Crimes Committed in Ethiopia

Eshetu Alemu is a dual Ethiopian and Dutch national who committed war crimes as a member of the communist military regime, the "Derg", that ruled Ethiopia from 1974-91.[42] Alemu settled in the Netherlands in 1990, a year prior to the Derg's collapse, and obtained Dutch citizenship in 1998.[43] Also in 1998, a Dutch magazine published an investigative piece linking Alemu to war crimes in Ethiopia.[44] Based on the magazine article, the Dutch National Investigative Service's International Crimes Unit opened what would become a four-year investigation into Alemu in 2009 that involved cooperation with Ethiopia's Special Prosecutor's Office.[45] Dutch police arrested Alemu in September 2015, and The Hague District Court convicted him of war crimes and sentenced him to life imprisonment in December 2017.[46] Alemu's case is to date the only prosecution abroad for international crimes committed in Ethiopia.

How frequently do States apply universal jurisdiction?

While most States have laws that allow them to investigate and prosecute crimes committed beyond their own borders, few have actually done so. This gap is due to a variety of procedural and practical limitations, including special requirements that can make it difficult to trigger jurisdiction, capacity issues among national authorities, and lack of political will. Underutilization of these laws represents a significant missed opportunity to pursue accountability on behalf of survivors and families of victims of atrocity crimes

The Gap in Application

Only 21 countries have launched at least one such case, despite -- States having laws enabling them to do so.

Even though -- U.N. Member States can hold alleged perpetrators of the most serious international crimes accountable even when the crimes were committed outside their borders, only about -- percent of those States have used their laws to open such cases. One of the key barriers that explains this gap is the legal limitations which effectively make universal jurisdiction much less universal than it was meant to be. Two such limitations are that many States require that the alleged perpetrator be present on their territory to open an investigation, and sometimes political authorization to initiate a prosecution.

Another challenge related to this gap is the perception it creates of the selective application of universal jurisdiction, and international criminal law in general. While the use of laws to investigate and prosecute extraterritorial crimes has been expanding geographically in recent years,[55] most serious international crimes cases (246 out of 270) have been opened in Europe against perpetrators from outside the continent.[56] This trend and the lack of prosecutions of suspected perpetrators from certain Western States undermine the universality of the international justice system.[57] Further geographical expansion of prosecutions is critically important for the system's future credibility and success.

Figure 5B: Specialized Units to Investigate and Prosecute International Crimes – -- U.N. Member States have a specialized unit

Beyond legal limitations, additional barriers to more widespread application of universal jurisdiction are lack of capacity and political will among national authorities. Investigations of the most serious international crimes often require more specialized expertise and resources than investigations of other offenses, and many States are hesitant to spend what limited resources they have on lengthy investigations of crimes that took place outside their borders. States may also face political pressure from other States not to carry out prosecutions at all.[58]

An increase in recent years in the number of specialized units dedicated to international crimes as part of police and prosecutorial teams is a sign of growing commitment among States to combatting impunity for the most serious international crimes. Additionally, collaborative platforms such as the European Network for the investigation and prosecution of genocide, crimes against humanity and war crimes within Eurojust have been created "to enable close cooperation between the national authorities [of EU Member States] when investigating and prosecuting" such crimes.[59] However, more work needs to be done to build this type of investigative and prosecutorial expertise outside of Europe, and to combat the lack of political will that often impedes the opening of serious international crimes cases in the first place.

Despite limitations and challenges, the possibilities for prosecuting alleged perpetrators of the most serious international crimes at the national level are significant and expanding. Developing and supporting this system will enable effective access to justice for thousands of survivors. It will also ensure that a wide range of perpetrators who are currently outside the reach of the International Criminal Court can still be brought to account.

Project Meridian provides comprehensive and transparent information to help survivors and practitioners make the most of these expanding opportunities. Equally, the Project has identified some of the main obstacles to realizing the promise of universal justice. The information collected and tracked by Project Meridian can help advocates, practitioners, and government officials turn this promise into a reality.
