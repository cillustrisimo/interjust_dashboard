Universal Jurisdiction

Jurisdiction over Serious International Crimes

Universal jurisdiction allows countries to prosecute the most serious international crimes—genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity, and aggression—regardless of where they were committed, the nationality of the perpetrator, or the nationality of the victim. This principle is crucial for ensuring that perpetrators have nowhere to hide and that survivors can access justice.

UN States with Laws to Investigate Crimes Beyond Their Borders

The total number of UN Member States that have legal frameworks allowing them to investigate and prosecute at least one of the four serious international crimes even when committed outside their borders.

Key Insight: 148 out of 193 UN Member States (164 jurisdictions) have laws that allow them to investigate and prosecute at least one serious international crime committed beyond their own borders.

1. Absolute Universal Jurisdiction

This means they can open a case against a person suspected of committing a serious international crime regardless of:

  • The nationality of the alleged perpetrator
  • The nationality of the victim
  • The location where the offence was committed
  • The presence of the suspect

Regional Breakdown - Absolute Universal Jurisdiction

Example: In Belgium, proceedings could be started without any clear link to Belgium. This broad authority was largely curtailed in 2003 after political pressure, but Belgium still retains absolute universal jurisdiction over genocide.

2. Conditional Universal Jurisdiction (Presence Required)

Key characteristics:

  • Suspect must be physically present in the country
  • No other connection to the country required
  • Often called "presence-based jurisdiction"
  • Most common form of universal jurisdiction

Regional Breakdown - Presence Required

Example: In 2016, a Rwandan man living in Germany was convicted by a German court for participating in genocide in Rwanda in 1994. The case was possible because the suspect was residing in Germany.

3. Active Personality Principle

This principle allows states to prosecute:

  • Their own citizens for crimes abroad
  • Regardless of where the crime occurred
  • Regardless of victim nationality
  • Also known as "nationality principle"

Regional Breakdown - Active Personality

Example: France prosecuted a French military officer for torture committed in Mauritania. The case proceeded based on the officer's French nationality, despite the crimes occurring abroad.

4. Passive Personality Principle

This principle allows states to prosecute:

  • When their citizens are victims
  • Regardless of where the crime occurred
  • Regardless of perpetrator nationality
  • Based on victim's connection to the state

Regional Breakdown - Passive Personality

Example: In 1994, families of Belgian peacekeepers murdered alongside Rwandan civilians during the Rwandan genocide initiated a case in Belgium against a former Rwandan officer. After many years of procedural hurdles, the suspect eventually turned himself in and was tried and convicted for war crimes in Belgium in 2007.

5. Protective Principle

This includes crimes deemed to affect:

  • National sovereignty
  • State security
  • National interests
  • Crimes committed against the state

Regional Breakdown - Protective Principle

Note: Few states recognize this type of jurisdiction and those that do rarely, if ever, invoke it to prosecute core international crimes. In 2022, Austria's Ministry of Justice issued a decree stating it would interpret its protective jurisdiction provision to extend to Ukrainian survivors of serious international crimes who fled to Austria following Russia's invasion.

6. Treaty-Based Jurisdiction

Most relevant treaties include:

  • 1949 Geneva Conventions - obligation to prosecute or extradite for "grave breaches"
  • Convention against Torture
  • International Convention for the Protection of All Persons from Enforced Disappearance

Important: Any country that has ratified the Geneva Conventions has not only jurisdiction but an obligation to investigate and prosecute individuals for "grave breaches" of the Conventions committed in other countries, or to extradite them to a country that is better placed to prosecute.
